Exhibit 9 · 1922–1938

Decline of Lehi Station

The roundhouse collapse, the fires, the Depression, and the end of the Iron Age in Lehi, Utah

  1. Home
  2. Exhibits
  3. Exhibit 9: Decline of Lehi Station (1922–1938)
Station A

The Incremental Decline

The decline of Lehi Station was not sudden but rather a slow, painful erosion over two decades, driven by a series of compounding pressures. The first major blow came in 1918 when the through-train service on the Salt Lake and Western line was discontinued, severing the most direct passenger connection to Salt Lake City.[81]

The early 1920s saw roadway competition intensify as more automobiles appeared on the new state highways, and the national economic pressure of the Great Depression hit the already-struggling rail lines hard throughout the decade. Each year, fewer freight shipments moved through the yard as the Tintic mining boom faded and the beet sugar industry mechanized.

Station B

The Roundhouse Collapse & Demolition

On July 28, 1922, the Lehi Junction roundhouse collapsed. The failure was dramatic and documented in local newspapers: a rail car loader exceeded the safe height clearance of the roundhouse roof, causing the entire structure to give way. This single event removed the operational heart of the Junction — the facility where locomotives were turned, serviced, and stored.[82]

What remained of the structural shell deteriorated throughout the 1920s and 1930s until it was finally demolished in 1937. Only a year later, in 1938, the Cutler Station stop was officially removed from the operational timetable, completing the erasure of the branch network that had once linked Lehi to the silver mines and beet fields of the Intermountain West.

In 1938, Cutler Station was removed from the timetable — ending over 60 years as one of Utah's most vital rail crossings.

The 1943 Station Fire

Just five years after Cutler Station's removal, what remained of the rail infrastructure suffered its final, catastrophic blow. In 1943, a coal dust fire swept through the remaining depot structure, leveling the building to its foundations. Coal dust, a highly combustible byproduct of decades of locomotive maintenance, had accumulated throughout the structure. A single spark was sufficient to end the depot's physical existence.[83] The fire marked the definitive, physical end of the active Iron Age at Lehi Junction.[84]

Station C

National Obsolescence

Lehi was not alone. Across the American West, hundreds of once-thriving rail hubs became "ghost stations" during this same period, their depots boarded up and their roundhouses silent.[85] Three forces, operating in concert, drove this nationwide obsolescence.

First, the Federal Aid Highway Acts of 1916 and 1921 created a national network of paved roads suitable for automobiles and commercial trucks, offering shippers flexibility and door-to-door delivery that railroad schedules simply could not match. Second, the nationwide expansion of the telephone network eliminated the railroad telegraph's monopoly on long-distance communication — the depot's role as the town's information hub simply ceased to exist. Third, the rise of the trucking industry, accelerated by World War II and the subsequent Interstate Highway System, transferred most of the remaining short-haul freight from rail to road.

In Lehi's case, this national narrative played out locally with painful precision. The same technological restlessness and drive for connectivity that had driven Brigham Young to build the Utah Southern Railroad now built highways and strung telephone wire.[86] The iron tracks did not just rust; they were replaced.
